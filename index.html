HumanMaterial: Physics and Neural-driven Full-body Human Material Estimation

*Anonymous

HumanMaterial aims to estimate Physically-Based Rendering (PBR) materials from a single human image. It enables the estimation of high-quality PBR materials with class differentiation, including normal, diffuse albedo (Diff.), roughness (Roug.), specular albedo (Spec.), ambient occlusion (AO), subsurface scattering (SSS) and depth map (Depth), all from a single input image. Leveraging these estimated results, our method facilitates realistic applications such as relighting and material editing under a novel environment map. Additionally, we employ gamma correction to enhance the brightness of diffuse albedo, specular albedo and subsurface scattering, thereby improving visual quality.

Abstract

We present a novel method, MaterialDiffusion, for estimating physically-based rendering (PBR) materials from a single human image. Previous approaches heavily rely on empirical models to depict human appearance, which poses limitations on accurate material estimation and application, particularly in the absence of PBR material datasets for training. In this way, relighting's robustness is limited, and skin lacks realism. To address these challenges, we construct the inaugural human PBR material dataset (OpenhumanBRDF) with class differentiation and devise a physics and neural-driven model (HumanMaterial). Specifically, we first produce a high-quality human material dataset (OpenhumanBRDF) based on the scanned real human data. Then, we propose HumanMaterial, which integrates physics-based and neural-driven approaches. It utilizes a PBR renderer to render the direct-light appearance and employ a neural renderer to handle the indirect-light appearance. By combining them, we achieve a realistic rendering of global-light appearance. Moreover, we design an Across-Depth-Scale Guidance Feature Fusion (AGFF) to enhance the accuracy of material estimation and introduce a multi-scene rendering loss to improve the robustness. At the same time, we design light-source-aware and foreground-aware loss to reduce the foreground artifacts of the estimated HDR environment map. Through extensive experiments on both our OpenhumanBRDF dataset and real data, we demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance. Furthermore, we can achieve realistic relighting and class-level material editing.
